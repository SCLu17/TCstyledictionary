# Disambiguation Dictionary for Traditional Chinese 類字辨
## Introduction
The words ‘traditional Han characters’ (traditional Chinese, old forms, _kyūjitai_) mean different things in different places: regional governments each have their own guidelines, let alone individual dictionaries or news agency manuals. All such documents will have their pros and cons in terms of their scope, selection principles, reasoning and encoding compatibility. This poses a problem for copywriters, proofreaders and others who have to work with copy set in it. Furthermore, regional variations mean that incomplete character sets in typefaces are a common issue for typesetters; this is where knowledge of variant unification leftovers in computer encodings becomes vital.

Hong Kong’s situation is particularly acute due to several reasons: its government being late to publish its Chinese-language guidelines, its comparatively narrow range, its characterization as a ‘guideline’ and not a ‘standard’, and Hong Kong’s own influences from other regions of China as well as Japan. All this combined makes Chinese language usage in Hong Kong highly variable and dynamic — a headache for the proofreaders, who would benefit greatly from a more complete guide.

The _Disambiguation Dictionary for Traditional Chinese_ is an attempt to aid editors in streamlining the proofing process, by collecting and comparing similar or alternative characters as used across regions of China, Japan and Korea, in a similar vein to style guides in foreign languages. Due to the unique nature of CJK ideograph implementation in computers, the dictionary is split into two parts: the first addresses ideographic forms and variants, the second addresses ideograph selection for compounds.

In addition to the dictionary’s PDF file, an ODS spreadsheet (suffixed with 'cheatsheet') is also enclosed with the release with all explanatory notes omitted, allowing users to quickly search for variants via e.g. regular expressions. Readers are welcome to make changes or additions to the spreadsheet and create their own style guide for use.

The expression ‘every book has its errata’ would be one familiar to Chinese readers. This is very much a solo project; suggestions and corrections are welcome via [Discussions](https://github.com/SCLu17/TCstyledictionary/discussions) or [Issues](https://github.com/SCLu17/TCstyledictionary/issues).

**NB**: This project is for reference only. The author has no intent of creating a ‘standard’ or any ‘authority’ regarding traditional Han character usage. Where preferences or first choices are stated, they should be taken literally, without any implication of ‘correctness’ and ‘incorrectness’ thereof. For the sake of brevity, this dictionary only collects common variations within and across regions, and is not intended to be an exhaustive volume.

## Known issues
- Improper text selection in the PDF release. This is a typesetting limitation on the software’s part when text is set vertically. No fix is planned for the near future, nor a horizontally-typeset version. This should not affect search functionality for individual characters.
